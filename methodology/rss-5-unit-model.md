# RSS 5-Unit Model

**Source:** RSS Trainer's Guide (2013), RSS Participant Manual (2002), RSS Facilitator's Guide (BlueLinx 2007), RSS Participant Manual (MMI 2012)
**Status:** Digitised from SharePoint -- awaiting David Warren validation via Methodology Lab

## Overview

Relationship Selling Skills (RSS) is a structured sales methodology built around five sequential units that guide a salesperson through a complete sales interaction: **Positioning, Discovering, Building, Presenting,** and **Resolving Concerns**. A sixth unit, **Securing**, addresses commitment but is outside the scope of the 5-Unit coaching model. [NEEDS DAVID WARREN REVIEW: Confirm whether Securing is excluded from the coaching platform or should be included as Unit 6.]

The five units follow the path of the **Situational Matrix** -- a flexible road map based on the customer's perception of needs and value. The progression logic is:

1. The salesperson begins in the **Low Needs / Low Value** quadrant, where the customer is unsure, indifferent, or unaware.
2. Through **Positioning**, the salesperson establishes purpose, credibility, and permission to proceed.
3. Through **Discovering**, both salesperson and customer arrive at a joint definition of the customer's needs (moving along the Needs axis).
4. Through **Building**, the customer's perception of impact, consequence, and urgency deepens (moving from Low Need to High Need on the matrix).
5. Through **Presenting**, the customer gains awareness of the value and benefits of the proposed solution (moving up the Value axis).
6. Through **Resolving Concerns**, obstacles are removed so the customer can reach the **High Needs / High Value** quadrant where commitment becomes natural.

The model is explicitly **not a rigid step-by-step process**. The salesperson must remain flexible, moving back and forth between units as the customer's perception shifts. The matrix applies both to a single sales call and to a long-term sales process spanning many meetings. In any given call, the salesperson may use skills from all five units.

The RSS philosophy is fundamentally different from traditional selling approaches (Formula, Barrier, Mood, and Need-Satisfaction selling). RSS focuses on:
- Working **with** the customer, not **on** the customer
- A full range of needs explored, not just those the product can meet
- Long-term relationship built on trust, not short-term transactional closing
- The salesperson as a problem-solver who adds value through **how** they sell

## Unit 1: Positioning

### Core Concept

Positioning is the process of moving from "just another salesperson" to a credible, trusted resource in the customer's mind. It establishes **who you are**, **what your product or service can do**, and **why this conversation is worth having**. Positioning occurs at the very beginning of a relationship and continues throughout the entire sales process.

The source material states: *"Experienced salespeople interviewed in the development of this program reported that more than 60 percent of their sales were lost on the first call."* First impressions are formed quickly and are difficult to reverse. The salesperson is the "intruder" into the customer's world and must earn the right to proceed.

Positioning tells the customer three things about you:
- **Your company** -- its market position, capabilities, and value proposition
- **Your product/service** -- general benefits relevant to the customer's world
- **Yourself** -- credibility, empathy, and expertise as an individual

### Techniques

**1. General Benefit Statement (GBS)**

The primary tool of Positioning. A GBS establishes the purpose of a call, meeting, or topic transition. It has three components:

- **Mention a relevant, possible need** (conversational approach, not specific)
- **Describe how you and your company have helped others** (examples, analogies)
- **Describe the process of interaction; request permission to proceed**

Example from source:
> "Everyone today is concerned about their company's profits. And rightly so with all the uncertainty in the economy. At [Company], we're totally committed to providing profitable solutions to companies like yours no matter what your circumstances. We've been doing it for almost 125 years with thousands of companies, helping them solve their [specific] needs. What I'd like to do today is to tell you a bit more about us and learn a bit more about your situation. By knowing more about your current circumstances and where you'd like to be in the future, we can probably come up with some ways to assure that your [solutions] are performing the way you want, both now and in the future."

GBS can also be used in phone calls, letters, and to set appointments -- any time a purpose needs to be established.

**2. Positioning Throughout the Process**

Positioning is not a one-time event at the start of a call. It is used:
- At the beginning of **any meeting** to establish purpose
- When **moving from one phase to another** (e.g., from Building to Presenting)
- When **moving from one topic to another**, or when a sensitive question needs context
- **Any time** the customer seems unsure where you are headed
- **Any time** you want to introduce a new topic

Example positioning statements from source:
> "You've given me a good picture of your needs. Now it would be useful to explore some of the ways we might respond or talk out some general approaches we might recommend to see if there's a fit."

> "Based upon what you've told me, I feel like I have a very accurate picture of your current situation and your goals. What I'd like to do is to spend some time with you in discussing what we found out and some possible recommendations."

**3. Pre-Call Planning**

Research the customer's company, industry, market position, and personnel before the first contact. Sources include business publications, trade publications, industry associations, annual reports, customer literature, and informal sources. Pre-call planning shows the customer you are invested and increases the chance of success.

**4. Style Management**

The four style factors that influence customer perception:
- **Empathy** -- People buy from people they perceive to be like themselves. Early in the process, empathy is the most critical factor.
- **Expertise** -- People buy from people they perceive to be experts. Illustrate credibility through name-drops and short examples.
- **Enthusiasm** -- People buy from enthusiastic salespeople, but early on, temper enthusiasm to avoid appearing pushy. Match the customer's energy level.
- **Ego** -- Can help (tenacity) or hinder (overriding the customer's agenda). Temper ego in early interactions.

The rule in Positioning: **Stress empathy, illustrate expertise, temper ego and enthusiasm.**

### Key Language and Behaviours

- Let the customer take the lead in setting the tone
- Search for common ground through "small talk" -- but be alert to cues
- Listen attentively; observe the customer's environment for "Hot Buttons" (individual needs and style preferences)
- Never assume anything about the customer's needs
- Avoid putting the customer on the defensive
- Use empathetic responses: *"I can understand how you might feel that way"*
- Give information to get information in return

### Situational Matrix Connection

Positioning operates primarily in the **Low Needs / Low Value** quadrant. The customer is unsure, indifferent, or unaware of how the salesperson, product, or company can help. Successful Positioning earns the right to move into Discovering.

Positioning also occurs at the start of every subsequent interaction, even if the overall sales process is well advanced -- each new meeting requires the salesperson to re-establish purpose and context.

### Progression Indicators

The salesperson knows Positioning has been achieved when:
- The customer understands the purpose of the conversation
- The customer grants permission to proceed (agrees to continue, schedules a follow-up, answers questions)
- The customer begins to share information about their situation
- A basis of trust and mutual respect has been established
- The customer moves from guarded/cautious to engaged

### Common Pitfalls

- **Too much enthusiasm too early** -- appears pushy and creates distrust
- **Letting ego override empathy** -- talking about yourself instead of listening to the customer
- **Skipping straight to product features** -- presenting benefits before understanding the customer's world
- **Ignoring the customer's agenda** -- sticking to your own plan instead of following the customer's lead
- **Failing to position throughout** -- only positioning at the start and never re-establishing purpose when transitioning between topics or phases
- **Poor first impressions** -- 60% of sales lost on the first call according to source research

## Unit 2: Discovering

### Core Concept

Discovering is a **two-sided** process in which both the salesperson and the customer arrive at a **joint definition of needs** that is specific, understood by both parties, and worth doing something about. The critical insight is that discovery is not one-sided interrogation -- it is a collaborative conversation.

The source states: *"What they often don't understand is that 'discovery' is two-sided, not one-sided... a process in which both the salesperson and the customer arrive at a joint definition of needs."*

The fundamental formula:
- **Instead of:** Problem -> Salesperson's Definition -> Solution
- **Do this:** Problem -> Joint Definition -> Solution

A key distinction in Discovering is the difference between a **problem** and a **need**:
- **A problem** is an existing condition that diverges from expectations (e.g., poor performance, poor image, poor financial standing)
- **A need** implies change, a desire to take action to correct a problem (e.g., increase performance, improve image, increase profits)
- **A need demands satisfaction; a problem doesn't always have to be solved**

The salesperson must convert problems into needs through confirmation before presenting any benefits.

### Techniques

**1. The Discovering Strategy (General to Specific)**

Discovery moves through four stages:

1. **Assumptions** -- You begin with basic assumptions about why the customer may need your product, based on experience, research, and pre-call planning.
2. **Situation** -- Gather general information about how the customer operates, their market position, organisational structure, and current approach. This verifies or denies your assumptions.
3. **Problems** -- As you understand the situation, explore clues to possible problems or areas where your product might fit. Help the customer explore these in depth.
4. **Needs** -- Confirm that the customer would like the problem solved and there is urgency, thereby converting the problem to a need.

**2. Open Questions**

The primary tool of Discovering. Open questions:
- Cannot be answered by "yes" or "no"
- Seek open-ended responses about facts, situations, problems, concerns, and feelings
- Seek clarification of customer statements
- Use words: **who, what, when, where, how, why**

Examples from source:
> "What are your financial objectives in the short term?"
> "What kinds of problems have you had?"
> "When do these problems happen?"
> "Tell me, how does your [team] feel about that?"
> "Why is that?"
> "Oh?"

**3. Closed Questions**

Used sparingly for two purposes: (1) confirming understanding and (2) regaining lost focus.

Examples:
> "Then, if I understand, the problem is short-term cash flow?"
> "So, what I'm hearing is that you'd like to deal with a company that could provide more than just [X]?"

**Improper use of closed questions** -- manipulative or directive:
> "Wouldn't you agree that...?"
> "If I could show you a way to save money, wouldn't you be interested?"
> "Haven't you had problems with your...?"

These are directive and assumptive, forcing a response favourable to the salesperson's pitch. Avoid them.

**4. Share / Provide Information**

A steady stream of questions without feedback is an interrogation. Discovering is a **conversational process** in which you share information and provide rationale for your questions.

- Position questions when you move from one topic to another
- Use examples and analogies to help the customer understand where you are going

Example:
> "You raise a good point. We've found that more and more people are taking steps to ensure that they have a second source supplier. That's an option we should maybe spend some time discussing. How do you feel about that?"

**5. Support**

Support builds a bridge of empathy. Guidelines:
- Look for areas of agreement and actively acknowledge the customer's feelings
- Agree with and acknowledge any comment that is useful
- Acknowledge the customer's right to feelings, even if negative
- Use the customer's own words (shows you are listening)

Examples from source:
> "You're right."
> "Absolutely. We find that to be the case in almost every situation."
> "You know, you're way ahead of most people in realising the need to plan for the future."

**6. Reamplification Paraphrase**

A technique for confirming understanding while restating with amplification:
> Customer: "Our current technology is out of date and our customers are demanding better quality."
> Salesperson: "So, if I understand you correctly, you need a way to provide better quality and keep within your budget restrictions."

### Key Language and Behaviours

- Ask open questions to explore; confirm with closed questions
- Support and acknowledge feelings, problems, opinions, and facts
- Share information -- maintain a conversational approach
- Do not present benefits to stated problems; present benefits only to confirmed needs
- Identify **all** needs before presenting any solutions -- gather the full picture first
- Discovering is a conversation, not an interrogation

### Situational Matrix Connection

Discovering operates along the **Needs axis**, moving the customer from Low Needs awareness toward High Needs awareness. Through joint exploration of situations, problems, and needs, both parties build a shared understanding of what needs to change.

### Progression Indicators

- Both salesperson and customer can articulate a shared, specific definition of needs
- The customer acknowledges that the identified needs are worth addressing
- Problems have been converted into needs through confirmation
- The full range of needs has been explored (not just the first one mentioned)
- The customer is actively sharing information and engaged in dialogue

### Common Pitfalls

- **One-sided discovery** -- interrogating rather than conversing
- **Presenting benefits prematurely** -- hearing a problem and immediately jumping to a solution before the need is jointly defined
- **Using too many closed questions** -- limiting the customer's ability to express themselves
- **Failing to distinguish problems from needs** -- assuming every stated problem is a need requiring action
- **Not gathering the full picture** -- addressing the first need mentioned instead of exploring all needs before building solutions
- **Ignoring the customer's perspective** -- defining needs from the salesperson's viewpoint rather than jointly

## Unit 3: Building

### Core Concept

Building is the process of deepening and expanding the customer's perception of need. It is the critical unit that moves the customer from **Low Need to High Need** on the Situational Matrix by helping them understand the full **impact and consequences** of their problems.

The source states: *"A common situation encountered in selling is the customer who is somewhat aware that a need exists, is at least partially aware that your product might be the answer but isn't quite ready to solve it."* Generally, the customer is not ready to act because:
- The need has not been totally defined in terms of impact
- There is no urgency in the customer's mind to address it

Building requires:
- Build the customer's awareness of the **impact or consequence** of the need
- Build knowledge of the **impact of a solution**
- Build a **sense of urgency** toward implementing it
- **Engage the customer in problem solving** to show impact

The fundamental principle of Building is **asking versus telling**. When a salesperson tells a customer they have a problem, the customer tends to reject it. But when the customer can **think through** a problem from different viewpoints, they accept the change in perception without difficulty.

**Building is an appropriate strategy when:**
- The customer has a partial awareness of needs
- The customer has a partial awareness of the benefits you can provide

**Building is NOT manipulation.** The source explicitly states: *"You are not trying to get acceptance of a premise or rationale that you know is a short-term solution in which you stand to gain more than your customer does. Instead, you are engaging in a problem-solving discussion."*

### Techniques

**1. Critical Questions**

The primary tool of Building. Critical questions are open-ended questions that cause the customer to **think about impact or consequences** -- to speculate, imagine, project, and analyse a situation.

Contrast with informational questions (which seek facts and figures):
- **Informational:** "What types of applications do you currently have?"
- **Critical:** "Imagine what kind of financial impact a failure in the field could have on your company?"
- **Informational:** "Have you ever had a Health and Safety audit?"
- **Critical:** "Think about the impact on your production situation if the line was totally shut down?"

Some questions are both critical and informational -- they request information but in the process cause the customer to think about scope.

**2. Projecting**

Help the customer understand potential impact by projecting forward:

> "If you could rely on getting the service that you need here in your plant, how would that make you feel?"
> "You'd feel this way. How would your employees feel?"
> "What are some of the long-range solutions you see that would solve this problem?"

**3. Calculating**

Quantify the impact in tangible, objective terms to help the customer evaluate both the problem and the solution:

> "We estimate we can save you 2 seconds in the production of each component. 15,000 components a day is equal to 8 hours per day of saved production."

From the case study dialogue:
> Bill: "So how many hours would that translate to per week for the average agent?"
> Bill: "If those thousand agents are spending 8,000 hours a week on administrative duties, how valuable would it be if that time could be cut in half?"
> Carl: "If we could free up 4,000 hours a week..."
> Bill: "Look at it another way. How long does one of your agent's average sales presentations take?"
> Carl: "About an hour... So if we could make time for another 4,000 presentations to customers every week..."

**4. Using Examples and Analogies**

Use examples to position the problem and let the customer think about the impact by drawing a mental picture:

> "Think about the difference between installing a [new system] versus the manual system you are currently using. This would allow you to produce the same quantity in two shifts instead of three."

**5. Handling the Indifferent Customer (Position-Build Strategy)**

When the customer has low perception of both needs and value (truly indifferent):

1. **Position first** -- The indifferent customer sees no need and will be unresponsive to questions about needs. Establish purpose and show you may be a resource.
2. **Be supportive, even complimentary** -- It is easier to get a person to talk about successes than problems.
3. **Avoid asking closed questions about problems** -- A satisfied customer does not see problems or will not admit them.
4. **Build involvement through support** -- The more you get a person to talk, the more likely you will spot a problem they do not see.
5. **Be prepared to build** -- The seeds of dissatisfaction lie in satisfaction. A small problem may be a major opportunity if you can build on the impact.

Example dialogue from source:
> Customer: "We don't really need another supplier. We've got a relationship with others who do all that for us now, and we're very happy."
> Salesperson: "That's good -- I'm glad you've been able to select a team that's doing a good job for you. That's more than a lot of others I know have been able to do. How did you go about doing it?"

As the customer describes their success, listen for partial problems:
> Customer: "Oh, it works great. We have suppliers who... But we do have an occasional problem with..."
> Salesperson: "Oh?"
> Customer: "Oh, it's nothing really. I mean nobody's perfect."
> Salesperson: "Tell me a bit more. There's an area where we might be able to help. What kinds of problems have you had, and how often do they occur?"

Now you are into Building and can explore the impact.

### Key Language and Behaviours

- Use open-ended critical questions that cause the customer to think about consequences
- Involve the customer in problem solving -- do not tell, ask
- Project, calculate, and use examples/analogies to make impact tangible
- Build with genuine concern and empathy
- Help the customer view reality from different vantage points
- Remember: "Don't tell the guy, let him find it"

### Situational Matrix Connection

Building drives the most significant movement on the matrix. It moves the customer along the **Needs axis from Low Need to High Need** by increasing awareness of impact, consequence, and urgency. When Building is effective, the customer shifts from "I have a minor issue" to "This is something I need to address now."

Building also operates when dealing with the **indifferent customer** in the Low Needs / Low Value quadrant, using a combined Position-Build strategy.

### Progression Indicators

- The customer articulates the impact of the problem in their own words
- The customer demonstrates a sense of urgency to act
- The customer begins to quantify the cost or consequence of inaction
- The customer moves from partial awareness to full understanding of need scope
- The customer says something like: "We need to find out what's going on" or "We absolutely have to change the way we handle this"

### Common Pitfalls

- **Telling instead of asking** -- asserting that the customer has a problem rather than helping them discover it through questions
- **Failing to quantify** -- leaving impact as an abstract concept rather than calculating tangible numbers
- **Moving to Presenting too early** -- jumping to solutions before the customer fully appreciates the scope of the need
- **Ignoring the indifferent customer** -- giving up when the customer says "everything is fine" instead of using the Position-Build strategy
- **Being manipulative** -- Building must be done with genuine concern, not to trick the customer into a short-term sale

## Unit 4: Presenting

### Core Concept

Presenting is the process of showing the customer how your solution addresses their discovered and built needs. The goal is not simply to sell a product but to **sell the right product and the best solution** by involving the customer in the evaluation.

The source states: *"We liked your product, but his seemed to be a better fit." Those words, heard too often, indicate a lost sale. Was the winning product a 'better fit' or did the salesperson do a better job in educating and working with the customer?*

**Presenting is an appropriate strategy when the customer has:**
- A high awareness of needs (achieved through Discovering and Building)
- Minimal awareness of the benefits you can provide

**The best solution is the one that works** -- not necessarily the least expensive, the most technologically advanced, or the flashiest. It is the one that fits best into how the customer operates, meets their priorities, and satisfies their perceived needs. The best solution consists of many factors: product benefits, service, delivery, cost, and technical support.

### Techniques

**1. Position the Transition to Presenting**

Before presenting, acknowledge that needs have been explored and signal the shift to solutions:

> "Mr. Customer, I think together we have a good understanding of your needs and priorities. What I'd like to do is show you a couple of ways we can do this. Is that OK?"

**2. Present Options (General to Specific)**

Move from general options to a specific recommendation:
- Present general options first to involve the customer
- Include pros and cons of each option -- be honest about drawbacks
- Encourage and support customer feedback on options
- Recommend the best solution

**3. Customer Involvement**

Customer involvement is critical in Presenting for three reasons:
1. Acceptance increases when the customer has a stake in a jointly developed solution
2. The customer is the best judge of what will or will not work in their environment
3. Objections surface as discussion points rather than obstacles when the customer is involved, allowing concerns to be resolved before they become objections

**4. Treat the Whole Problem**

Avoid the "premature introduction of product benefits" trap where salespeople present a benefit every time a need is mentioned. Consequences of this approach:
- The solution may not be right since the customer only sees salesperson-selected positives
- The full range of needs may never be understood; the solution may lack important ingredients
- Customer resistance increases, creating tough objections the salesperson has manufactured

**5. Be Honest About Pros and Cons**

> "They're not the least expensive suppliers, but I know what I'm getting when I buy from them. They never let me down."
> "They understand the way we like to do business. When they sell me something, I know it's going to work."

### Key Language and Behaviours

- Maintain customer involvement throughout
- Summarise needs, options, and possible solutions
- Anticipate and resolve concerns before they become obstacles
- Propose or recommend the best solution
- As you work with the customer to define the best solution:
  - **Demonstrate expertise**
  - **Echo enthusiasm** (match the customer's energy)
  - **Show empathy**
  - **Temper ego**

### Situational Matrix Connection

Presenting operates along the **Value axis**, moving the customer from Low Value awareness to High Value awareness. The customer already has high need awareness (from Discovering and Building) but does not yet understand how the salesperson's solution can help. Presenting bridges this gap.

### Progression Indicators

- The customer understands the full range of options available
- The customer can articulate how the proposed solution addresses their needs
- The customer has weighed pros and cons and is comfortable with the recommended approach
- The customer demonstrates ownership of the solution ("our plan" rather than "your proposal")
- Concerns surface as discussion points, not blockers

### Common Pitfalls

- **Premature presentation** -- presenting benefits before needs are fully discovered and built
- **Feature-centred rather than need-centred** -- talking about product features instead of connecting to the customer's specific discovered needs
- **Forcing a solution** -- not involving the customer in evaluation, creating resistance
- **Hiding drawbacks** -- not being honest about pros and cons, which erodes trust
- **Presenting too many options** -- overwhelming the customer instead of guiding toward the best solution
- **Selling the product instead of the relationship** -- the product may be only part of the total solution; service, delivery, and technical support add value

## Unit 5: Resolving Concerns

### Core Concept

Resolving is the process of addressing customer concerns -- doubts, misinformation, and legitimate objections -- in a way that preserves the relationship and advances the sale. The RSS methodology explicitly rejects the term "overcoming objections" because it implies an adversarial posture.

The source states: *"'Overcoming' implies that your task is to put down or minimize an objection, that you 'overcome' your customer as you would 'overcome' an adversary. Keep discord or conflict out of the picture."*

Concerns are a **natural and healthy** part of the customer's evaluation process. They are **indications of interest** -- if the customer is serious about making a decision, concerns need to be resolved before an informed purchase decision can be made.

### Concern Categories

The source identifies three categories of concern:

**1. Doubt**
- **Source:** The customer does not believe that your product or organisation can provide an important benefit
- **Tactic:** Provide evidence to prove that it can

**2. Misinformation**
- **Source:** The customer has incorrect information about an important aspect of your company or product
- **Tactic:** Provide correct information

**3. Legitimate Concern**
- **Source:** The customer perceives some aspect of your product as a genuine drawback or problem
- **Tactic:** Help the customer weigh the concern against all of the benefits you can provide

### Resolution Techniques

The source provides a 5-step resolution framework:

**Step 1: UNDERSTAND the concern BEFORE you respond**
- Ask open-ended questions to clarify your understanding
- LISTEN -- do not interrupt -- let the customer talk

**Step 2: CONFIRM your understanding**
> "If I understand, you're concerned that...?"

**Step 3: ACKNOWLEDGE the customer's right to the concern**
> "I can understand why that would be important to you..."

**Step 4: ANSWER -- by providing proof, information, pros/cons/options**
- For **Doubt**: provide evidence (case studies, references, data)
- For **Misinformation**: provide correct information (gently, without making the customer feel foolish)
- For **Legitimate Concern**: help the customer weigh the concern against the full range of benefits
- If an explanation is needed, be concise

**Step 5: CHECK for understanding and acceptance**
> "Does that make sense? Have we resolved that issue?"

### Key Language

- Approach concerns with empathy, respect, and understanding
- Do not create obstacles -- listen, do not interrupt or assume
- Provide a straightforward answer to information requested; do not treat every question as an objection
- Make sure you understand what the customer is asking before you respond -- if you are not sure, ask

**Questions do not always mean concerns.** Customers ask many questions; some indicate concern, others simply request information. The source warns against the common mistake:
> Customer: "How much is it?"
> Salesperson: "It's $X. Now, that's less than what you'd have to pay if... I know it's more than you thought, but..."

This defensive response creates concern where none existed. Simply answer the question.

### Situational Matrix Connection

Resolving operates in the transition zone toward **High Needs / High Value**. The customer already has high need awareness and some understanding of value, but concerns represent friction that prevents movement into the commitment zone. Resolving removes these barriers.

### Progression Indicators

- The customer's concerns have been identified and articulated
- Each concern has been addressed with the appropriate tactic (evidence for doubt, correct information for misinformation, weighing for legitimate concerns)
- The customer confirms that concerns have been resolved
- The customer's overall evaluation shows benefits heavily outweighing drawbacks
- The customer is ready to move toward commitment

### Common Pitfalls

- **Treating concerns as adversarial** -- using "overcoming" tactics instead of collaborative resolution
- **Creating your own objections** through high-pressure tactics:
  - Attempting to force ideas too early
  - Disputing or ignoring a concern
  - Treating concerns as minor: "Don't worry. Trust me."
  - Attempting to gain commitment prematurely / trial closing
- **Jumping on the first words** -- responding before fully understanding the concern
- **Treating questions as objections** -- becoming defensive when the customer simply wants information
- **Minimising legitimate concerns** -- every concern, no matter how trivial it seems, is important to the customer and deserves respect
- **Not checking for acceptance** -- assuming the concern is resolved without confirming with the customer

## Unit Progression and the Situational Matrix

The five RSS units map directly to movement on the Situational Matrix:

```
                    High Value
                        |
         Presenting     |     SECURING
         (educate on    |     (commitment)
          value)        |
                        |
  Low Needs -------- BUILDING -------- High Needs
                        |
         Positioning    |     Resolving
         (establish     |     (remove
          purpose)      |      barriers)
                        |
                    Low Value
```

**Movement patterns:**

| Unit | Primary Movement | From | To |
|------|-----------------|------|-----|
| Positioning | Establishes entry point | Outside the matrix | Low Needs / Low Value (engaged) |
| Discovering | Horizontal (Needs axis) | Low Needs | Toward High Needs (needs jointly defined) |
| Building | Horizontal (Needs axis) | Partial Need awareness | High Needs (impact understood, urgency created) |
| Presenting | Vertical (Value axis) | Low Value | High Value (solution benefits understood) |
| Resolving | Clears barriers to commitment | High Needs / partial Value | High Needs / High Value (concerns resolved) |

**Key principles of matrix movement:**

1. The pattern generally follows an "N" shape (Positioning -> Discovering -> Building -> Presenting -> Securing) but the salesperson must remain flexible.
2. The matrix applies to **both** the overall sales process and to **each individual meeting** within that process.
3. Even in a first meeting, the salesperson may use skills from all units -- positioning, some discovering, some building, and may secure a commitment to a next meeting.
4. Conversely, even late in the process, the salesperson may need to reposition at the start of a new meeting or return to discovering if new needs emerge.
5. It is the **customer's perception** of needs and value that determines where on the matrix you are and what skills to use. The salesperson follows the customer's lead.
6. Building is the critical differentiator -- it is what transforms a customer from "I see a minor issue" to "I need to act now," driving the most significant movement on the Needs axis.
7. Resolving is not a failure state -- concerns are natural and indicate the customer is seriously evaluating the purchase.

**The Indifferent Customer (special case):**

When the customer is firmly in Low Needs / Low Value with no perceived problems, the standard Discovering strategy will not work because the customer has nothing to discover. The strategy is:
1. **Position** -- establish purpose and show you may be a resource
2. **Build directly** -- support the customer's satisfaction, get them talking about successes, listen for partial problems, and build on those

This bypasses standard Discovering because the indifferent customer will not engage in needs discussion until they see a problem worth exploring.
