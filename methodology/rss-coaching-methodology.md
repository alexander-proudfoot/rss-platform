**Source:** Bowater RSS Coaching Manual (Proudfoot Consulting, December 2006)
**Status:** Digitised from SharePoint -- awaiting David Warren validation via Methodology Lab

## Overview

The RSS Coaching Manual defines coaching as **"proactively guiding individuals and teams to achieve successful results."** Coaching is framed as the critical reinforcement mechanism after RSS training: without ongoing coaching, salespeople retain only about 20% of what they learned during the RSS programme. The manual positions the sales manager as the primary coach, responsible for continuous development of sales personnel through routine observation in the field.

The coaching philosophy draws on the "catalyst-coach" model: unlike a traditional manager who relies on close supervision and tight control, the catalyst-coach focuses on creating an environment in which people want to succeed and providing them with the tools they need to succeed. The catalyst-coach provides information, insights, resources, reinforcement, and ongoing support.

## Core Coaching Philosophy

The manual establishes the following foundational principles:

### What Makes a Good Coach (Catalyst-Coach Model)

A catalyst-coach **does**:
- Encourage people to develop their own approaches
- Continue coaching and supporting someone who falters or makes mistakes
- Create a climate in which it is acceptable to learn from mistakes
- Thoroughly plan how to coach and support the person or team taking on a responsibility
- Ensure success in the end by recognising and reinforcing successes along the way
- Provide developmental feedback in a supportive way
- Look to the team and organisation for sources of coaching and support

A catalyst-coach **does not**:
- Insist on having his or her own way
- Take over or give the responsibility to someone else
- Punish mistakes
- Thoroughly plan how the person or team should handle the responsibility (i.e., dictate the approach)
- Take good performance for granted or think people don't need recognition or reinforcement
- Criticise and manage by exception
- Consider himself or herself the only coach for the group

### Traits of a Good Coach

From Ken Blanchard and Don Shula ("The Little Book of Coaching"):
- **Conviction-driven:** Never compromise your beliefs
- **Overlearning:** Practice until it's perfect
- **Audible-Ready:** Know when to change
- **Consistency:** Respond predictably to performance
- **Honesty-based:** Walk your talk

### Why Managers Should Coach

Coaching provides the **opportunity** to know:
- The employee as an individual
- The individual areas of expertise and levels of expertise
- The work accomplishments
- Job conditions and environment
- The customers -- what they want; what they think; and how expectations are changing

With that first-hand knowledge, management will be more effective in:
- Providing timely, specific, knowledgeable and accurate feedback and effective recognition to the right salespeople, at the right time, for the right reasons
- Speaking up authoritatively in problem-solving sessions
- Providing knowledgeable input to project teams and task forces

### Key Points to Remember

Coaching:
- Is the responsibility of management at all levels
- Is an aspect of an individual's development
- Recognises and encourages good performance
- Shows individuals that the manager really cares
- Results in both individual and team development
- Results in retention

Coaching will:
- Teach salespeople to identify their developmental opportunities
- Improve an individual's motivation
- Clarify goals

Coaching responsibilities range from holding scheduled, structured coaching sessions to informal situations as a need is identified, including giving on-the-spot guidance and praise. Everyone needs coaching, even top performers.

## Four-Step Continuous Coaching Process

[NEEDS DAVID WARREN REVIEW: This is referred to as the "C2E Framework" in the task brief. The source document calls it "a four-step, continuous process" without a branded name. Please confirm the correct naming.]

The core coaching framework is a four-step continuous cycle:

### 1. Observe
**Observe** what the person is doing.

Much of a manager's time with salespeople will be spent making observations. There are many elements of the job that can be observed simultaneously:
- Knowledge of processes and departmental functions
- Technical quality
- Effective use of systems, tools, and effective work habits
- Product quality
- Professionalism -- professional relationships with co-workers and customers
- Working with others -- the manager, co-workers, workers in other departments as well as customers
- Economic use of tools and materials
- **Most important: Sales Skills in front of the customer**

### 2. Think
**Think** about the standards, requirements or level of expertise to be attained.

What is this person doing and how are their actions compared to requirements or standards for this process?

### 3. Decide
**Decide** what coaching is needed.

What coaching is needed, how can coaching help this person and the work group, what is it this person can or should be doing?

### 4. Act
**Act**, which could be either:
- **Sustain behaviour** by praising the current performance
- **Change behaviour** by taking developmental or corrective action

Initiate a discussion and determine a coaching plan with the individual.

## The Coaching Process (Session Structure)

The manual defines a structured coaching process with five sequential phases:

```
Training --> Coaching Start-up --> Pre-call Planning --> Sales Call Observation --> Post-call Feedback --> Performance Improvement Action Plan
```

The process repeats as a continuous cycle. Each phase is described below.

### Phase 1: Coaching Start-up

**Position the coaching process** with salespeople, either in a group session or one-on-one:
- Explain coaching and its benefits as an ongoing process, not a "quick fix" gimmick
- Explain clearly that this coaching process **is not an evaluation**, but a joint effort to enhance and support the skills of the entire organisation
- Frame it as a continuing developmental effort that will benefit all

**Responsibilities:**
- The coaching calls are the **primary responsibility of the salesperson**
- The **salesperson controls the call**; the manager is mainly an **observer**
- This is not a joint call where both would be actively involved
- The customer's focus should remain on the salesperson -- they have to call on the customer again
- Common courtesy and common sense dictate the manager's level of involvement

**Call criteria:**
- Appointments should involve **active selling**, not simply a courtesy call to drop off something
- They should be **low-risk situations** where the manager's participation, even if problems develop, should not be required
- If the manager is to function effectively as an observer, they cannot be worried about rescuing a floundering high-stakes call

### Phase 2: Pre-call Planning

**Schedule in advance:**
- With the salesperson, select a suitable day to spend together in the field
- Make clear it is for the **whole day** with **multiple sales calls**
- The salesperson should set up appointments in advance
- Allow sufficient time between calls to discuss observations
- An entire day allows coaching between calls and observing how the salesperson performs at the next opportunity

**Call preparation** (leave all initiatives to the salesperson; observe preparedness):
- Does the salesperson have a plan for the day?
- Is he/she prepared with collateral material, etc.?
- On each call planned, has he/she anticipated some areas of need?
- Does the salesperson have a clear call objective?

**Pre-call planning checklist:**
- Review call preparation
- Review account information
- Discuss anticipated needs
- State call objective / fallback objective
- Review how to introduce manager to the customer

**Key rule:** The salesperson controls the call; the manager observes.

Review the Coaching Feedback Record with the salesperson, explaining it is not a test or an evaluation. Explain that you will note the skills used and the strengths and weaknesses observed during the sales call and refer to it when providing post-call feedback.

### Phase 3: Sales Call Observation

**During the call:**
- As a courtesy, let the customer know you will be taking some notes
- Be as **unobtrusive** as possible
- Control the note-taking -- this is not dictation
- Jot down key points only
- Do not try to record every word, just words that will act as reminders for later discussion

**Use the Coaching Feedback Record** and key words to reconstruct the call later.

**Focus on consistency:**
- Highlight attributes and skills that are displayed **consistently** -- either effective or ineffective
- Do not pick on every little detail, as it would be confusing and very de-motivating
- Thank the customer before leaving

**Sales call observation checklist:**
- Position note-taking with customer
- Record the observations
- Thank the customer

### Phase 4: Post-call Feedback

**The importance of praise:**
Identifying shortcomings and explaining what must be improved are a coach's responsibility, however, praising individuals for their improvement and good performance is **even more important**. Sincere and honest praise shows that their efforts are appreciated. Praise should be a part of every coach's normal day-to-day leadership action. Successful coaches use praise effectively and often.

**Timing and location:**
- Post-call feedback should occur **outside the customer's facility**
- Preferably where coach and salesperson can talk freely and comfortably
- Should take place **as soon after the sales call as possible** (while the call is fresh)
- The salesperson will also be able to implement skill improvements on the next call

**The process is a conversation, not a critique.**

Both coach and salesperson provide feedback. The coach mentions skills used **particularly well first**, then discusses skills not used well or not used at all.

**Post-call feedback process (four steps):**

1. **Get salesperson's opinion:**
   - "What skills did you use, things that you did, on the call that went particularly well?"
   - "Are there any areas where you think you could have done better?"
   - The salesperson's feedback may or may not reflect your view, but treat it as useful and be supportive of the process

2. **Review the RSS Model:**
   - In discussing the call, always refer to the RSS model (the Situational Matrix) to diagnose what went on and provide feedback
   - Ask: "Where do you think the customer was in terms of perception of needs and value?"
   - Ask: "What things did the customer say or do that made you feel that way?"
   - Ask: "What did you do in response?"
   - Use the model to provide feedback based on the salesperson's answers

3. **Provide your feedback based on observations:**
   - What did you like
   - What areas to improve
   - Customer's reaction

4. **Develop action steps to take between now and the next call:**
   - Role play in the car
   - Review the questions to be asked
   - "Let me show you how I would have handled that situation..."
   - Discuss other options
   - Whatever the action steps, **get commitment from the salesperson to try the new behaviour in the next call**

**Common problems to observe:**
- Didn't position properly
- Doesn't have a complete knowledge of the customer's situation and so should have asked more questions
- Assumes he/she has heard a need expressed and presents too early
- Didn't use critical questions to build
- Didn't seek commitment or lay out next steps in securing
- Didn't attempt to secure the sale

**Key principle:** Even if the call was technically perfect, using the model to review serves to reinforce the sales process until it becomes a way of life in your organisation.

### Phase 5: Improvement Actions and Follow-up

**End-of-day review:**
At the end of the day, sit down with the salesperson and review observations:
1. Support and enhance areas where the salesperson has demonstrated improvement and skill
2. Identify areas of improvement needed and develop an action plan

**Establish the performance objective** by referring to the Coaching Feedback Record. For example:
- "He said... and you responded by asking this..."
- "Why do you think you're having problems getting the customer actively involved?"

**Ask for and suggest improvement actions:**
- "What do you think you can do about it?"
- Do role reversals based on the call (coach becomes the salesperson; salesperson is the customer) -- this allows the salesperson to see things from the customer's point of view
- Ask the salesperson to review specific sections in the RSS participant manual, then follow up with a one-on-one discussion or group session

**Confirm/agree on the action plan(s):**
- The salesperson will work on specific improvements
- Schedule to meet **within a week** to evaluate how effectively the proposed changes are working
- Agree on a date for the **next round of coaching calls**

**Improvement actions checklist:**
- Establish cause of performance problem
- Ask for / suggest improvement actions
- Confirm / agree on action plans

**Group coaching:** If a number of salespeople are having problems in common, consider a group training session before or after office hours. These sessions should include role-plays too.

**Follow-up:** Coaching does not end after a coaching session. The coach must act on or follow up on what was discussed. Ensuring that the proper actions are being taken and that the right things are happening contributes to the continued success of the salesperson and the coach.

## Observation Protocols

### What to Observe During a Sales Call

The Coaching Feedback Record provides a structured observation framework aligned to the RSS units. Each skill area is rated on a 1-6 scale.

**Call Preparation:**
- How well did the salesperson define an objective for the call?
- How well did the salesperson prepare for the call?
- How well did the salesperson use the benefit statement?
- How well was the preparation used to build trust and confidence in the meeting?

**Communication:**
- How well did the salesperson align with the style of the customer?
- How well did the salesperson show empathy for the customer's situation throughout the meeting?
- How well did the salesperson actively listen to the customer throughout the meeting?
- How well did the salesperson secure each step of the sales process and review actions required?

**Positioning:**
- How well did the salesperson build rapport and establish a purpose with the customer?
- How well did the salesperson establish a rationale for proceeding?
- How well did the salesperson show the client where the discussion was headed?

**Discovering:**
- How well did the salesperson use open-ended and closed questions to gain knowledge of the customer's situation?
- How well did the salesperson help the customer explore the situation, problems and needs?
- How well did the salesperson arrive at a mutual understanding and definition of needs?

**Building:**
- How well did the salesperson use critical questions in building?
- How well did the salesperson build the awareness of the impact or consequence of the need?
- How well did the salesperson build the customer's knowledge of the impact and value of a solution?

**Presenting:**
- How well did the salesperson educate the customer on the options available and the pros and cons of each option?
- How well did the salesperson have the customer take a stake in the solution they developed jointly?

**Resolving:**
- How well did the salesperson confirm and acknowledge the customer's concerns?
- How well did the salesperson resolve doubt, misunderstanding, or a legitimate concern?

**Securing:**
- How well did the salesperson request the customer's commitment?
- How well did the salesperson support and reinforce the customer's decision?
- How well did the salesperson outline what is going to happen after the sale?
- How well did the salesperson ask for referrals?

### Observation Principles

- Be a capable and careful observer
- Record the sales call on the Coaching Feedback Record by checking the appropriate areas and using brief notes to help reconstruct the call later
- Highlight **consistencies** of skill use: effective and ineffective
- The coach's role is to look for and correct consistent inappropriate behaviour and to look for and praise consistent appropriate behaviour
- One call is not representative -- anybody can look good on one call only to revert to bad habits afterwards

### RSS Coaching Feedback Form (D-G-E Scale)

The manual also includes a simplified coaching feedback form using a three-point scale:
- **D** = Needs Development
- **G** = Good
- **E** = Excellent

This form covers all RSS phases plus before-the-call and after-the-call activities:

**Before the Call:**
- Thoroughly completed and used the Call Planning Document
- Reviewed customer information with me before the call

**Positioning:**
- Presented a Positioning / General Benefits Statement
- Positioning / General Benefits Statement was successful
- Established a purpose for the meeting with the customer

**Discovering:**
- Used open-ended questions, gave support and examples as needed
- Actively listened and explored the customer's situation, problems, needs
- Succeeded in identifying the customer's needs
- Confirmed needs through the use of closed questions

**Building:**
- Used critical questions to help the customer realise his/her needs and find solutions
- Succeeded in getting the customer to expose his/her problem/opportunity

**Presenting:**
- Suggested solutions / options
- Presented pros and cons of the solutions presented

**Resolving:**
- Listened, acknowledged, and confirmed the customer's concerns
- Asked questions in order to understand the problem or concern
- Answered customer's concerns without minimising them

**Securing:**
- Summarised the needs expressed
- Summarised the solutions presented, asked for commitment
- Identified and discussed the next steps (who, what, when, where)
- Identified and discussed the time and objective of the next call

**After the Call:**
- Updated the customer's information
- The objective(s) of the call were achieved
- Reviewed and analysed the call and the critical steps
- Defined his/her action plan for future action

## Feedback Frameworks

### Principles of Effective Feedback

Feedback is a form of communication which lets the coach know whether or not activities are effective. It is an essential ingredient in problem identification and problem solving. For feedback to be most effective, it must be **factual**. The person giving the feedback must exercise self-control and communicate in a way the other person will perceive as helpful.

**When asking for and receiving feedback:**
- Want it
- Be willing to accept help
- Be willing to be influenced by the help
- Accept feedback as being the person's honest perception
- Be interested in building a stronger, more productive relationship with the person giving the feedback
- Never react defensively or argue about the information being given
- Realise that it may be difficult for the giver
- Make it clear that we value the feedback which we receive

**When giving feedback:**
- Believe that the person wants an outside check on their behaviour, or prepare the person to hear unsolicited feedback
- Be honest, "on the level"
- Give feedback to help the person receiving it, not to be vicious or to hurt the person
- Never argue to defend observations
- Take the risk of really helping, rather than being superficial

### Guidelines for Effective Feedback Sessions

- Be prepared
- Be specific
- Deal with facts
- Get both sides
- Do not personally attack the other person; discuss only the situation at hand
- Maintain control of the situation by keeping the focus on the issue at hand; do not drift and discuss other people or other subjects
- Address their reasons, overcome their excuses
- Do not be afraid
- Accept your responsibility
- Discuss your options along with the advantages and disadvantages of each one
- Have clear goals and keep your objective in mind
- Be aware of and react to non-verbal signals
- Remain calm
- Avoid absolute statements
- Listen to what they have to say
- Make decisions by consensus
- Provide a means for their input and involvement in the solution

## Coaching Cadence

The manual specifies the following cadence elements:

- **Coaching day:** A full day spent together in the field with multiple sales calls
- **Scheduling:** Coaching days are scheduled in advance with the salesperson
- **Between-call coaching:** Sufficient time between calls to discuss observations and coach
- **Post-call feedback:** Immediately after each call, outside the customer's facility
- **End-of-day review:** A sit-down review at the end of the coaching day to develop improvement action plans
- **Follow-up meeting:** Scheduled **within a week** to evaluate how effectively proposed changes are working
- **Next coaching round:** A date for the next round of coaching calls is agreed at the end of each session
- **Feedback record cycle:** A new Coaching Feedback Record is started every four to five call session

## Coaching Conversation Techniques

### Unit-Specific Coaching Tips

The manual provides practical coaching techniques organised by RSS unit:

**Positioning:**
- Ask salesperson to write out a general benefit statement for the next call
- Practice a GBS in the car on the way to the next appointment
- Review the Social Styles Matrix and relate it to the customer just called on
- Practice a smooth transition from Discovering to solution Presenting

**Discovering:**
- *Open questions:*
  - Ask salesperson to prepare ten open questions to use in exploring a customer's situation
  - Role play questions in the car between appointments
  - Prepare a list of questions for the next call
  - Model questions on one call -- ask the salesperson to use similar ones on the next call
- *Closed questions:*
  - In the car, have the salesperson ask an open-ended question, then answer and have the salesperson confirm using a closed question
  - Have the salesperson develop a list of confirming phrases:
    - "Let me make sure I understand... you said..."
    - "What I'm hearing is..."
    - "I think you're saying that..."
    - "Then the problem/need is..."
- *Support:*
  - Practice support statements in the car (coach says something that the salesperson then supports)
  - On a call, interrupt and support the customer's comments a few times to show the salesperson a live example

**Building:**
- Review the Building unit in the workbook
- Develop a list of common critical questions; have the salesperson carry the list for a week and check them off after use on calls
- Model them yourself on a live call -- interrupt the call to ask a critical question, review later with salesperson
- Develop a list of phrases that introduce critical questions:
  - "How would you feel if..."
  - "Imagine what would happen if..."
  - "How much more income would be available if..."
  - "What would you change about... if you could?"

**Presenting:**
- Develop a list of options you could present
- Role play the pros and cons of these options in the car
- Review the workbook
- Model the strategy on a call -- present the options yourself, then discuss later with the salesperson

**Resolving:**
- Develop a list of common concerns
- Role play in the car AND at every sales meeting
- Make the "price of admission" to a sales meeting: how well the salesperson resolves a concern posed to them at the door
- Encourage salespeople to share a difficult concern they dealt with that week; discuss various ways to resolve that concern
- Review concerns, doubts, misinformation that come up after each call and how they were resolved

**Securing:**
- After each call, review if the salesperson asked for a commitment and how
- Review each close -- did the salesperson review Next Steps completely?
- Did the salesperson attempt to secure a commitment at the "right" time -- was it too early or too late?
- If the close was not successful, did the salesperson find out why? Did he/she attempt a second close (if appropriate)?

### Role Reversal Technique

A specific technique described in the manual: after observing a call, the coach and salesperson swap roles. The coach becomes the salesperson and the salesperson plays the customer they have just called on. This allows the salesperson to see things from the customer's point of view, which can be very effective.

## Reactions and Pitfalls

### Common Reactions to Coaching

**Nervousness:** The individual may appear nervous and overly sensitive. The coach should explain the reason for the session to eliminate uncertainty. Begin by discussing something the individual has done well or a familiar subject. Giving the person a chance to respond may help relieve tension.

**Cooperation:** Most people react positively to the guidance and assistance of a competent coach. Most are willing to participate and accept suggestions for improvement.

**Relationship disagreement:** People may not always agree with criticism. Disagreement can be rational and unemotional. Misunderstanding needs to be clarified. Once the person understands, there may still be disagreement -- this can be helpful to developing self-reliance.

**"Too easy" agreement:** The salesperson may agree completely and almost too easily. This may show a lack of understanding or indifference. The coach must ensure the salesperson is truly agreeing, rather than trying to avoid more criticism or confronting a problem.

**Determination to argue:** Some people may seem determined to argue or disagree with any evaluation. This may indicate the problem is not what it appears to be. Encourage them to talk freely while you listen to determine the real problem. Calmly ensure the salesperson understands your point of view.

**Attempts to shift blame:** A person may seek to avoid blame by shifting criticism to others, superiors, or even to policy. Inform the salesperson that all circumstances will be verified. Guide the discussion and assist by providing specific information to substantiate each claim. The individual should be required to take responsibility for personal action.

**Loss of temper:** The coach should listen, not argue, and try to find what caused the loss of temper. Give the person time to recover and continue, or postpone the session until the salesperson is ready.

### Coaching Pitfalls to Avoid

**Personal bias:** Personal values influence priorities. If differences between personal values are ignored, facts can become distorted and problems further complicated.

**Rash judgments:** The tendency to evaluate others on the basis of appearance or specific behaviour trait. A halo effect may come from a significant accomplishment or favourable first impression (or the reverse from one bad impression). Always keep the problem in mind and challenge evaluations based on one factor or observation.

**Stereotyping:** Judging others on presumed group, physical or behavioural characteristics. Evaluations should be made only on an individual's demonstrated behaviour or abilities.

**Loss of emotional control:** If the coach's emotions are controlled, the individual is led to do the same. Arguing, debating, and heated discussions waste time, upset the individual and weaken the ability to think clearly.

**Inflexible methods:** People vary according to their individual personalities, experience, education, problems, situations and surroundings. The same coaching technique or approach will not be effective for all. You must know each individual and be flexible in your approach.

**Amateur character analysis:** A good coach must recognise and accept personal limitations. Simple problem solving, providing facts and evaluating job performance are within range. Do not try to determine or change deep-seated personality disorders. Coaches must identify situations that are clearly beyond their capabilities and refer the person to the appropriate resource.

**Improper follow-up:** Promises not kept to one person will cause the loss of confidence and respect of others on the team.

**Reluctance to coach:** Inexperienced managers may hesitate to coach on areas of improvement or want to avoid discussing shortcomings for fear of becoming unpopular. Without the coaching effort, the problem will only get worse.

## Using the RSS Units as Coaching Lens

The manual explicitly ties coaching observation and feedback to the five RSS units (Positioning, Discovering, Building, Presenting, Resolving) plus Securing. The RSS Situational Matrix serves as the diagnostic framework during post-call feedback.

### How the RSS Units Inform Coaching

1. **During observation:** The Coaching Feedback Record is structured around the RSS units. The coach observes and rates the salesperson's performance in each unit during the live call.

2. **During post-call feedback:** The coach and salesperson review the call using the RSS model (Situational Matrix). The key diagnostic questions are:
   - "Where do you think the customer was in terms of perception of needs and value?"
   - "What things did the customer say or do that made you feel that way?"
   - "What did you do in response?"

3. **During improvement planning:** Improvement actions are tied to specific RSS units. Coaching tips are organised by unit (Positioning, Discovering, Building, Presenting, Resolving, Securing).

4. **During follow-up:** The coach uses the RSS model as a common language to reinforce the sales process until it becomes a way of life in the organisation.

The manual states: "Whatever the case, even if the call was technically perfect, using the model to review serves to reinforce the sales process until it becomes a way of life in your organisation."
