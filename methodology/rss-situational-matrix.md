# RSS Situational Matrix

## Overview

The Situational Matrix is the foundational framework of the Relationship Selling Skills (RSS) programme. It teaches salespeople to view the sales process from the customer's point of view, analyse the sales situation, and adopt appropriate skills and tactics to secure a commitment. The matrix provides a "road map that tells you where you are and where you have to go to advance the sale." It is explicitly described as a "situational model" — not a rigid step-by-step procedure.

The goal of the framework is to help the salesperson enhance the quality of each contact call in the sales cycle, develop a strong working relationship with the customer, and ultimately increase sales results. The matrix applies both to the overall sales process (whether that process occurs over a few minutes or over the course of many meetings) and to each individual encounter within that process.

## The Two Dimensions

### Dimension 1: Customer Perception of Need

Customers buy because they perceive a need. Perception is reality — if the customer perceives something to be real, then to them, it is real. A need is defined as "something that is lacking — a problem that requires action, an existing condition that can't be allowed to continue, a product or service that is required."

Customer perception of need exists on a spectrum from low to high:

- **Low Need:** The customer does not perceive a significant need or problem. At this end, no need or a very small need exists, so the customer probably will not act.
- **High Need:** The customer perceives a significant need or problem. At this end, the customer will probably act to satisfy the need.

Not all customers perceive the same needs or perceive needs with the same intensity. Perception of needs varies from customer to customer, and from situation to situation. The salesperson's role is to help the customer see the full impact of his or her needs.

### Dimension 2: Customer Perception of Value (Benefits)

Customers do not buy products or "features" of a product. They buy for the value — what the product will do for them to satisfy a need (save money, increase performance, perform a necessary function, make work easier, etc.). As the source states: "the person who buys a drill really wants or needs a hole, not a drill."

Customer perception of value also exists on a spectrum from low to high:

- **Low Value:** The customer does not yet see how the salesperson's product or solution can help them meet their needs. It is particularly difficult for customers when there are several products that all claim to have the same benefits.
- **High Value:** The customer clearly sees how the salesperson's product or solution addresses their needs and provides distinct value.

## The Four Quadrants

### Quadrant 1: High Need, High Value (upper right)
**Need perception:** High
**Value perception:** High

**Customer position:** The customer sees and understands both the need and the value of the salesperson's product or solution.

**Customer behaviours:** The customer is engaged, leaning forward, involved. They recognise they have a problem or opportunity and can see how the salesperson's offering addresses it.

**What the customer says/thinks:** The customer acknowledges both the problem/need and sees the value of the proposed solution. They are ready to move forward.

**Salesperson focus:** Close or secure commitment. Since the customer sees and understands both the need and value, the salesperson should move to secure the order.

**Primary RSS units:** Unit 6 — Securing (arriving at mutual commitment and obtaining the order).

### Quadrant 2: High Need, Low Value (lower right)
**Need perception:** High
**Value perception:** Low

**Customer position:** The customer has high need awareness but knows little about the value the salesperson's product will provide, or the customer sees value but cannot distinguish the salesperson's product from competitors' products.

**Customer behaviours:** The customer recognises they have a problem but is unconvinced about the specific solution being offered, or sees multiple options as interchangeable.

**What the customer says/thinks:** "I know I need something, but I'm not sure your product is the right one" or "How is yours different from the others?"

**Salesperson focus:** Educate the customer about the value. Explain what makes your product better than someone else's product. Present options and recommend the best solution for the customer's needs.

**Primary RSS units:** Unit 4 — Presenting (finding the best possible solution for the customer's needs); Unit 5 — Resolving (non-adversarial response to concerns — doubt, misinformation, or legitimate concerns).

### Quadrant 3: Low Need, Low Value (lower left)
**Need perception:** Low
**Value perception:** Low

**Customer position:** The customer has both a low perception of needs and value. This is often the starting position with new prospects or customers who do not yet recognise they have a problem.

**Customer behaviours:** The customer may appear disinterested, even bored. They do not see any pressing problems and have no particular awareness of the salesperson's product or its value.

**What the customer says/thinks:** "I don't see any problems" or "Things are fine as they are" or "I'm not sure why we're meeting."

**Salesperson focus:** First establish a purpose for talking through "positioning" — earning the right to continue the conversation. The salesperson can talk about their product and its benefits by describing how it has helped someone in a similar business or situation. It would be difficult to ask the customer about problems because this customer probably does not see any problems. The salesperson needs to create a need for the product by making the value so appealing that the customer perceives they just cannot do without it.

**Primary RSS units:** Unit 1 — Positioning (lets the customer know who you are and where you are going); Unit 2 — Discovering (joint definition of needs — using open and closed questions to explore the customer's situation, problems and needs).

### Quadrant 4: Low Need, High Value (upper left)
**Need perception:** Low
**Value perception:** High

**Customer position:** The customer sees the value or appeal of the product/solution but does not currently perceive a strong need for it. ## Movement Dynamics

### The "N" Pattern

The default movement pattern through the matrix traces the letter "N":

1. **Positioning** (Low Need, Low Value) — establish purpose and earn the right to continue
2. **Discovering** (moving from Low Need toward High Need) — jointly define needs using open and closed questions
3. **Building** (centre of the matrix) — help the customer look at problems and needs from different vantage points; create urgency
4. **Presenting** (High Need, Low Value moving toward High Value) — discuss options and recommend the best solution
5. **Resolving** (addressing concerns that arise) — non-adversarial response to objections
6. **Securing** (High Need, High Value) — arrive at mutual commitment and obtain the order

### Flexibility Is Essential

The "N" pattern is not a rigid procedure. Key principles of movement:

- **The customer determines your position:** "The factor that determines how you handle the call is the customer and wherever he or she is on the matrix. It is the customer's perception of need that determines what you do."
- **Move back and forth:** "You should be able to move back and forth from one element of the model to another by following your customer's lead."
- **Every encounter contains all elements:** Even a first meeting may involve some discovering, building, presenting, and securing — even if you are "securing nothing more than permission to continue the relationship."
- **Every meeting restarts positioning:** Even if you have met a customer many times, at the beginning of a new meeting you will need to position — alert the customer to the purpose of this meeting and establish a rationale for proceeding.
- **The matrix applies at multiple levels:** It applies to the overall sales process (across many meetings) and to each individual encounter within that process.

### What Causes Movement

- **From Low Need to High Need:** The salesperson helps the customer see the full impact of their needs through discovering (open and closed questions exploring situation, problems, needs) and building (critical questions about impact and consequence that create urgency to act).
- **From Low Value to High Value:** The salesperson presents options, explains benefits, and demonstrates how the product/solution specifically addresses the customer's defined needs, distinguishing it from competitors.
- **Regression is possible:** Customer perception of needs may change during the process. The salesperson must remain flexible and be prepared to move back to earlier elements of the model.

## Using the Matrix for Agent Coaching

An AI coaching agent should use the Situational Matrix in the following ways:

1. **Diagnose position before prescribing action:** Before suggesting what a salesperson should do, determine where the customer currently sits on the matrix based on the salesperson's description of the customer's behaviour and statements.

2. **Ask about both dimensions independently:** Assess the customer's perception of need and perception of value separately. A customer may be high on one dimension and low on the other.

3. **Guide toward the appropriate RSS unit:** Once the quadrant is identified, direct the salesperson to the skills and tactics from the corresponding RSS unit(s) rather than defaulting to a generic "close the sale" approach.

4. **Challenge premature movement:** If a salesperson is trying to present solutions (Unit 4) when the customer is still in a Low Need position, coach them to step back to Discovering (Unit 2) or Building (Unit 3) first.

5. **Reinforce flexibility:** Remind salespeople that the matrix is situational. If the customer's perception shifts during a call, the salesperson should follow the customer's lead rather than rigidly following the "N" pattern.

6. **Apply at both macro and micro levels:** Use the matrix to assess both the overall sales process (where is this deal across multiple meetings?) and the individual call (where should this specific conversation focus?).

7. **Use customer language as diagnostic input:** What the customer says and how they behave are the primary indicators of their position on the matrix. Coach salespeople to listen for cues that reveal perception of need and perception of value.
